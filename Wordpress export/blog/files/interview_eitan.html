Aninterview with Eitan Glinert for www.amirdotan.com/blog - Developingaccessible computer games


1. Tell us a bit about your background(academic/professional) and
related interests.

 

I started out inMIT in computer science and biology. School was a bit of a pressure cooker, andwhen I graduated I decided to get as far away from CS and bio as I could. Iwound up on Capitol Hill looking for work, but in a weird twist of fate endedup getting offered a job making a video game based on immunology with theFederation of American Scientists. I had to take it - how often do you getoffered the chance to make video games for a living? The topic was right up myalley too, teaching high school students immunology to get them more interestedin science and biology. So despite my best efforts I ended up using what Ilearned in school after all.


2. What is the Singapore-MIT GAMBIT Game Lab andhow did you began
your association with it?

 

The Singapore-MITGAMBIT Game Lab (http://gambit.mit.edu) isa brand new lab that is entirely devoted to making new and inventive videogames. The Singaporean government has decided that they want to bring the videogame industry to their country, and they've partnered with MIT to create a newlab to train that next generation of innovators. The really exciting part ofthe program is that as part of an academic institution we aren't afraid to trybizarre, untested hypotheses to see if they float. This summer, for instance,we're developing 6 new games with over 40 undergraduates from MIT and Singaporejust to test out some theories. At the end of the summer we should have 6prototypes.

 

My involvementwith the program was a combination of knowing the right people and having theright background. I was talking to Henry Jenkins (www.henryjenkins.org), the head of theComparative Media Studies department at MIT, about my immunology game and hesaid I should check out the new GAMBIT program he was starting up. They neededsomeone with game making experience, and I wanted to get my graduate degree,so√â here I am, I suppose.

3. How did you end up developing accessiblecomputer games?

It began inFebruary. I knew I wanted to do something new with video games, so I sat downwith my advisor, Eric Klopfer, and Philip Tan, the director of GAMBIT, to talkabout what limitations video games currently have. After a while we startedtalking about who can't play games, and what games were accessible to peoplewith disabilities. Specifically, there's a huge percentage of visually impairedpeople in the world (2.76% of Americans aged 16-64, for example) that can'treally use games. So they asked me if I thought it would be possible to make agame that a blind person can use meaningfully.

 

I like difficultproblems, and this one seemed too interesting to pass up. Over time the goalsshifted from a game for the blind to a game for everyone that is accessible tovisually impaired users, but that core focus of bringing people into videogames who can't normally play has never changed.


4. To what extent, is accessible game developmentdifferent than game
development for non-disabled gamers?

 

It's wildlydifferent! Normally, the whole point of developing a game is to make somethingfun. The second you start adding in extra requirements (educational content,multiplayer elements, accessibility) you drastically increase the designworkload and complicate your job. That's why making these kind of games is sohard.

 

The biggestdifficulty we've found is in designing the user interface. You need it to beeasy to understand and pick up, but have a lot of depth and allow you to docomplex actions. If the UI doesn't work, nothing in your game will. So wedecided to start thinking about that from the beginning, and see what kind ofgame we can make that will fit with that design.

5. Do you think disabled gamers and non-disabledgamers can share the
same rewarding gaming experience together using an inclusive game, or
do special needs almost always mean 'special games'?

Absolutely, andthat's entirely the point of what we're doing now. Look at it this way - thereare non-computer gamesthat both groups can play together, right? For instance, blind and sightedusers could play 20 questions or chess together, and they would have the sameexperience. Deaf and hearing users can play poker or a board game. So why can'tvideo games do the same thing?

 

They can, andthey will. The game we're making now is specifically designed to give the sameexperience to both blind and sighted users, and actually allows them to playtogether in a multiplayer mode. Hopefully, we'll design the game well enoughthat player A won't be able to tell if player B is sighted or not.

 

I suspect that asthe game industry matures and we see more innovation from companies likeNintendo we'll see more of this. If they can bring women and the elderly intogaming, surely the disabled will follow.


6. The gaming industry is a multi-billion industrywhich is doing extremely well. Where do accessible computer games fit in allthat gaming frenzy? How much demand is there for these games at the moment inyour opinion? Do you predict an increase in the future?

Accessible gamesfit into the "new" market segment. Around 10 years ago games were mostly thedomain of young men and teenaged boys. Nowadays, especially with the advent ofcasual games and "accessible" controllers like the Wii's, you see more and morewomen and elderly people getting into gaming. And that's huge for the industry,as it translates into billions in revenue. So as you can see, getting intothese new markets is extremely valuable for game companies.

 

As of 2000, 18.5%of Americans aged 5 upwards have some form of disability (source: US 2000census). Now, to be fair some of these disabilities don't affect one's abilityto play a game - if you're missing a leg, you might have no problem playingHalo. That said, the majority of mainstream video games are inaccessible to largeparts of the disabled population. A multi-billion dollar industry can't reallyafford to ignore this group, and I believe that as games become more and moreubiquitous we'll see an increase in the number and diversity of accessiblegames.


7. How do you ensure the end product appeals to thetarget audience?
Are any blind or partially sighted gamers involved in the development
process and conceptual stages?

That's somethingthat all game developers need to worry about; will their product resonate withtheir audience? In our case we have to be especially careful as we have novisually impaired individuals on our team. To help with this we're runningtesting every other week with local blind people to see if we're on the righttrack. We've also been talking over design with an MIT graduate who happens tobe blind, but her involvement in the project is minimal - all the more reasonwhy testing is critical.


8. In your opinion, what are the main differencesbetween testing a game with a disabled and non-disabled gamer?

You have to bevery aware of the games controls and their effect on the game. When you aretesting, is the tester struggling with the game, or with the user interface?What parts of the UI are difficult to use? The other difference is the matter ofwhat is obvious and what isn't obvious to which users. If we have a visualcomponent in our game with a corresponding audio cue, we need to be sensitivethat both are equally noticeable to both groups - it wouldn't do us much goodif one side picked up on it while the other was oblivious.


9. What are the main challenges when developing agame for blind or
partially sighted gamers?

Relying on audiois the hardest part. There are certain things that sight just does really well- displaying static text, for instance, or allowing "look ahead" so you knownot only what is immediately in front of you, but what is going to come in thenext small bit of time. Spatial navigation also works much better visually.When you try to do these things with audio, the result always feels strange andalien, at least to sighted users.

 

Our solution,therefore, was to NOT include these elements in our game, and focus instead onwhat audio does better than visualizations. That's why we're making a DJ game;music always works well with audio, and doesn't require a visual component.


10. You are currently working on a computer gamefor users with visual
impairments. Tell us about that project if you can.

Sure, I'll tell you what I can. The game is being developed by myself and a teamof 7 extremely talented undergraduates from MIT and Singapore universities.It's a DJ game, and will allow the user to step behind the turntables. It's amultiplayer game so both groups can play together and share that similarexperience we were talking about. The best part though, in my opinion, is thecontrol scheme - we're hooking a wiimote up to the computer so that you canreally use intuitive controls. The prototype will be finished by the end ofthis summer, at which point we'll post it for free download. I'll also beposting intermittent updates on www.eitanglinert.com,so be sure to check that out as well.
